Big idea of this course

fff is holomorphic   ⟺  \iff⟺ fff has complex derivative.

fff is holomorphic   ⟺  \iff⟺ fff satisfies Cauchy-Riemann equations ∂u∂x=∂v∂y\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}∂x∂u​=∂y∂v​ and ∂u∂y=−∂v∂x\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}=-\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}∂y∂u​=−∂x∂v​

fff is holomorphic   ⟺  \iff⟺ fff is analytic (is locally given by power series). The power series is integrable/differentiable term by term in the radius of convergence.

Convergence

Types of convergence

Converge pointwise (Not very strong):

∀x∈X,lim⁡n→∞fn(x)=f(x)\forall x\in X, \lim_{n\to\infty}f_n(x)=f(x)∀x∈X,limn→∞​fn​(x)=f(x).

Or, ∀x∈X,∀ϵ>0,∃N>0,∀n≥N  ⟹  ∣fn(x)−f(x)∣<ϵ\forall x\in X, \forall \epsilon>0, \exists N>0, \forall n\geq N \implies |f_n(x)-f(x)|<\epsilon∀x∈X,∀ϵ>0,∃N>0,∀n≥N⟹∣fn​(x)−f(x)∣<ϵ.

Converge uniformly (Much better):

∀ϵ>0,∃N>0,∀n≥N  ⟹  ∀x∈X,∣fn(x)−f(x)∣<ϵ\forall \epsilon>0, \exists N>0, \forall n\geq N \implies \forall x\in X, |f_n(x)-f(x)|<\epsilon∀ϵ>0,∃N>0,∀n≥N⟹∀x∈X,∣fn​(x)−f(x)∣<ϵ.

Converge locally uniformly (Strong):

∀x∈X\forall x\in X∀x∈X, ∃\exists∃ open x∈Ux\in Ux∈U, such that fn→ff_n\to ffn​→f uniformly on UUU.
