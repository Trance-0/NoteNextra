Information theory

Transmission, processing, extraction, and utilization of information.

  • Information: “Resolution” of uncertainty.
  • Question in the 1940’s: How to quantify the complexity of information?
    • Questions:
      • How many bits are required to describe an information source?
      • How many bits are required to transmit an information source?
      • How much information does one source reveal about another?
    • Applications:
      • Data Compression.
      • Channel Coding.
      • Privacy.
  • Claude Shannon 1948: Information Entropy.

Entropy

The “information value” of a message depends on how surprising it is.

  • The more unlikely the event, the more informative the message.

The Shannon information of an event EEE:

I(E)=−log⁡21Pr(E)I(E)=-\log_2 \frac{1}{Pr(E)}I(E)=−log2​Pr(E)1​

Entropy the the expected amount of information in a random trial.

  • Rolling a die has more entropy than rolling a coin (6 states vs 2 states).

Information entropy

Let XXX be a random variable with values in some finite set X\mathcal{X}X.

The entropy H(X)H(X)H(X) of XXX is defined as:

H(X)=∑x∈Xlog⁡21Pr(X=x)=−Ex∼X[Pr(X=x)log⁡2Pr(X=x)]H(X)=\sum_{x\in \mathcal{X}} \log_2 \frac{1}{Pr(X=x)}=-\mathbb{E}_{x\sim X}[Pr(X=x)\log_2 Pr(X=x)]H(X)=x∈X∑​log2​Pr(X=x)1​=−Ex∼X​[Pr(X=x)log2​Pr(X=x)]

Idea: How many bits are required, on average, to describe XXX?

  • The more unlikely the event, the more informative the message.
  • Use “few” bits for common xxx (i.e., Pr(x)Pr(x)Pr(x) large).
  • Use “many” bits for rare xxx (i.e., Pr(x)Pr(x)Pr(x) small).

Notes:

  • H(X)=Ex∼X[I(X=x)]H(X)=\mathbb{E}_{x\sim X}[I(X=x)]H(X)=Ex∼X​[I(X=x)]
  • H(X)≥0H(X)\geq 0H(X)≥0
  • H(X)=0H(X)=0H(X)=0 if and only if Pr(X=x)=1Pr(X=x)=1Pr(X=x)=1 for some x∈Xx\in \mathcal{X}x∈X.
  • Does not depend on X\mathcal{X}X but on the probability distribution Pr(X=x)Pr(X=x)Pr(X=x).
  • Maximum entropy is achieved when the distribution is uniform. H(Uniform(n))=log⁡2nH(Uniform(n))=\log_2 nH(Uniform(n))=log2​n. Where nnn is the number of events. (aaa bits required to describe each event of size 2a2^a2a).

Example

For uniform distribution X∼Uniform{0,1}X\sim Uniform\{0,1\}X∼Uniform{0,1}, we have H(X)=log⁡22=1H(X)=\log_2 2=1H(X)=log2​2=1.

For Bernoulli distribution X∼Bernoulli(p)X\sim Bernoulli(p)X∼Bernoulli(p), we have H(X)=−plog⁡2p−(1−p)log⁡2(1−p)=H(p)H(X)=-p\log_2 p-(1-p)\log_2 (1-p)=H(p)H(X)=−plog2​p−(1−p)log2​(1−p)=H(p).

Motivation

Optimal compression:

Consider the XXX with distribution given belows:

The average length of the encoding is 1/8×3+1/4×3+1/4×2+1/2×1=7/41/8\times 3+1/4\times 3+1/4\times 2+1/2\times 1=7/41/8×3+1/4×3+1/4×2+1/2×1=7/4.

And the entropy of XXX is H(X)=1/8×log⁡28+1/4×log⁡24+1/4×log⁡22+1/2×log⁡21=7/4H(X)=1/8\times \log_2 8+1/4\times \log_2 4+1/4\times \log_2 2+1/2\times \log_2 1=7/4H(X)=1/8×log2​8+1/4×log2​4+1/4×log2​2+1/2×log2​1=7/4.

So the average length of the encoding is equal to the entropy of XXX.

This is the optimal compression.

This is the Huffman coding.

Few extra theorems that will not be proved in CS course

  • Theorem: Avg. # of bits in any prefix-free compression of XXX is ≥ H(X)H(X)H(X).
  • Theorem: Huffman coding is optimal.
    • I.e., Avg. # of bits equals H(X)H(X)H(X).
  • Disadvantage of Huffman coding: the distribution must be known.
    • Generally not the case.
  • [Lempel-Ziv 1978]: Universal lossless data compression.

Conditional and joint entropy

How does the entropy of different random variables interact?

  • As a function of their dependence.
  • Needed in order to relate:
    • A variable and its compression.
    • A variable and its transmission over a noisy channel.
    • A variable and its encryption

Definition for joint entropy

Let XXX and YYY be discrete random variables.

The joint entropy H(X,Y)H(X,Y)H(X,Y) of XXX and YYY is defined as:

H(X,Y)=−∑x∈X,y∈YPr(X=x,Y=y)log⁡2Pr(X=x,Y=y)H(X,Y)=-\sum_{x\in \mathcal{X}, y\in \mathcal{Y}} Pr(X=x, Y=y) \log_2 Pr(X=x, Y=y)H(X,Y)=−x∈X,y∈Y∑​Pr(X=x,Y=y)log2​Pr(X=x,Y=y)

Notes:

  • H(X,Y)≥0H(X,Y)\geq 0H(X,Y)≥0
  • H(X,Y)=H(Y,X)H(X,Y)=H(Y,X)H(X,Y)=H(Y,X)
  • H(X,Y)≥max⁡{H(X),H(Y)}H(X,Y)\geq \max\{H(X),H(Y)\}H(X,Y)≥max{H(X),H(Y)}
  • H(X,Y)≤H(X)+H(Y)H(X,Y)\leq H(X)+H(Y)H(X,Y)≤H(X)+H(Y) with equality if and only if XXX and YYY are independent.

Conditional entropy

Note

Recall that the conditional probability P(Y∣X)P(Y|X)P(Y∣X) is defined as: P(Y∣X)=P(Y,X)P(X)P(Y|X)=\frac{P(Y,X)}{P(X)}P(Y∣X)=P(X)P(Y,X)​.

What is the average amount of information revealed by YYY given the distribution of XXX?

For each given x∈Xx\in \mathcal{X}x∈X, the conditional entropy H(Y∣X=x)H(Y|X=x)H(Y∣X=x) is defined as:

H(Y∣X=x)=−∑y∈Ylog⁡21Pr(Y=y∣X=x)=−∑y∈YPr(Y=y∣X=x)log⁡2Pr(Y=y∣X=x)\begin{aligned} H(Y|X=x)&=-\sum_{y\in \mathcal{Y}} \log_2 \frac{1}{Pr(Y=y|X=x)} \\ &=-\sum_{y\in \mathcal{Y}} Pr(Y=y|X=x) \log_2 Pr(Y=y|X=x) \\ \end{aligned}H(Y∣X=x)​=−y∈Y∑​log2​Pr(Y=y∣X=x)1​=−y∈Y∑​Pr(Y=y∣X=x)log2​Pr(Y=y∣X=x)​

The conditional entropy H(Y∣X)H(Y|X)H(Y∣X) is defined as:

H(Y∣X)=Ex∼X[H(Y∣X=x)]=−∑x∈XPr(X=x)H(Y∣X=x)=−∑x∈X,y∈YPr(X=x,Y=y)log⁡2Pr(Y=y∣X=x)=−∑x∈X,y∈YPr(x)∑y∈YPr(Y=y∣X=x)log⁡2Pr(Y=y∣X=x)\begin{aligned} H(Y|X)&=\mathbb{E}_{x\sim X}[H(Y|X=x)] \\ &=-\sum_{x\in \mathcal{X}} Pr(X=x)H(Y|X=x) \\ &=-\sum_{x\in \mathcal{X}, y\in \mathcal{Y}} Pr(X=x, Y=y) \log_2 Pr(Y=y|X=x) \\ &=-\sum_{x\in \mathcal{X}, y\in \mathcal{Y}} Pr(x)\sum_{y\in \mathcal{Y}} Pr(Y=y|X=x) \log_2 Pr(Y=y|X=x) \\ \end{aligned}H(Y∣X)​=Ex∼X​[H(Y∣X=x)]=−x∈X∑​Pr(X=x)H(Y∣X=x)=−x∈X,y∈Y∑​Pr(X=x,Y=y)log2​Pr(Y=y∣X=x)=−x∈X,y∈Y∑​Pr(x)y∈Y∑​Pr(Y=y∣X=x)log2​Pr(Y=y∣X=x)​

Notes:

  • H(X∣X)=0H(X|X)=0H(X∣X)=0
  • H(Y∣X)=0H(Y|X)=0H(Y∣X)=0 when YYY is a function of XXX.
  • Conditional entropy not necessarily symmetric. H(X∣Y)≠H(Y∣X)H(X|Y)\neq H(Y|X)H(X∣Y)=H(Y∣X).
  • Conditioning will not increase entropy. H(X∣Y)≤H(X)H(X|Y)\leq H(X)H(X∣Y)≤H(X).

Chain rules of entropy

Joint entropy: H(X,Y)=Ex∼X,y∼Ylog⁡1Pr(X=x,Y=y)H(X,Y)=\mathbb{E}_{x\sim X, y\sim Y}\log \frac{1}{Pr(X=x, Y=y)}H(X,Y)=Ex∼X,y∼Y​logPr(X=x,Y=y)1​.

Conditional entropy: H(Y∣X)=Ex∼Xlog⁡1Pr(Y=y∣X=x)H(Y|X)=\mathbb{E}_{x\sim X}\log \frac{1}{Pr(Y=y|X=x)}H(Y∣X)=Ex∼X​logPr(Y=y∣X=x)1​.

Chain rule: H(X,Y)=H(X)+H(Y∣X)H(X,Y)=H(X)+H(Y|X)H(X,Y)=H(X)+H(Y∣X).

Proof

For statistically independent XXX and YYY, we have Pr(x,y)=Pr(x)Pr(y)Pr(x,y)=Pr(x)Pr(y)Pr(x,y)=Pr(x)Pr(y).

Pr(y∣x)=Pr(x,y)Pr(x)=Pr(x)Pr(y)Pr(x)=Pr(y)Pr(y|x)=\frac{Pr(x,y)}{Pr(x)}=\frac{Pr(x)Pr(y)}{Pr(x)}=Pr(y)Pr(y∣x)=Pr(x)Pr(x,y)​=Pr(x)Pr(x)Pr(y)​=Pr(y).

Apply the symmetry of the joint distribution.

Mutual information

Definition for mutual information

The mutual information I(X;Y)I(X;Y)I(X;Y) of XXX and YYY is defined as:

I(X;Y)=H(X)−H(X∣Y)=H(Y)−H(Y∣X)I(X;Y)=H(X)-H(X|Y)=H(Y)-H(Y|X)I(X;Y)=H(X)−H(X∣Y)=H(Y)−H(Y∣X)

Properties of mutual information

  • I(X;Y)≥0I(X;Y)\geq 0I(X;Y)≥0

  • I(X;Y)=H(X)+H(Y)−H(X,Y)I(X;Y)=H(X)+H(Y)-H(X,Y)I(X;Y)=H(X)+H(Y)−H(X,Y)

Applications

Channel capacity

Recall a channel is tuple of (F,Φ,Pr⁡)(F,\Phi,\operatorname{Pr})(F,Φ,Pr).

Definition of discrete channel

A discrete channel is a system consisting of a discrete input alphabet X\mathcal{X}X, a discrete output alphabet Y\mathcal{Y}Y, and a probability transition Pr⁡(y∣x)\operatorname{Pr}(y|x)Pr(y∣x).

The channel is memoryless if the output at any time depends only on the input at that time and not on the inputs and outputs at other times.

Definition of channel capacity

The channel capacity CCC of a channel is defined as:

C=max⁡x∈XI(X;Y)C=\max_{x\in \mathcal{X}} I(X;Y)C=x∈Xmax​I(X;Y)

where I(X;Y)I(X;Y)I(X;Y) is the mutual information between XXX and YYY.

Shannon’s noisy coding theorem

Recall the rate of the code is R=log⁡∣F∣∣C∣nR=\frac{\log_{|F|}|\mathcal{C}|}{n}R=nlog∣F∣​∣C∣​.

For a discrete memoryless channel with capacity CCC, every rate R<CR<CR<C is achievable.

  • There exists a sequence of codes of length nnn and size ∣F∣nR|F|^{nR}∣F∣nR with vanishing probability of error as n→∞n\to \inftyn→∞.
  • In 1 channel use, it is impossible beat the noise.
  • In nnn channel uses as n→∞n\to \inftyn→∞,it is possible to beat the all the noise.

Corollary for the binary symmetric channel

The capacity of the binary symmetric channel with crossover probability ppp is 1−H(p)1-H(p)1−H(p).

Proof

H(Y∣X=x)=−Pr⁡(y=0∣x)log⁡2Pr⁡(y=0∣x)−Pr⁡(y=1∣x)log⁡2Pr⁡(y=1∣x)=−plog⁡p−(1−p)log⁡(1−p)=H(p)\begin{aligned} H(Y|X=x)&=-\operatorname{Pr}(y=0|x)\log_2 \operatorname{Pr}(y=0|x)-\operatorname{Pr}(y=1|x)\log_2 \operatorname{Pr}(y=1|x) \\ &=-p\log p-(1-p)\log (1-p)\\ &=H(p) \end{aligned}H(Y∣X=x)​=−Pr(y=0∣x)log2​Pr(y=0∣x)−Pr(y=1∣x)log2​Pr(y=1∣x)=−plogp−(1−p)log(1−p)=H(p)​

So,

\begin{aligned} H(Y|X)&=\sum_{x\in \mathcal{X}} \operatorname{Pr}(x)H(Y|X=x)\\ &=H(p)\sum_{x\in \mathcal{X}} \operatorname{Pr}(x) \\ &=H(p)

So,

\begin{aligned} I(X;Y)&=H(Y)-H(Y|X)\\ &\leq 1-H(p)
