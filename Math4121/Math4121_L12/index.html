Chapter 7: Uniform Convergence and Integrals

Our goal is to solve problems like this:

Let

sn,m=mm+ns_{n,m}=\frac{m}{m+n}sn,m​=m+nm​

The different order of computation gives different results:

lim⁡n→∞lim⁡m→∞sn,m=1\lim_{n\to\infty}\lim_{m\to\infty}s_{n,m}=1n→∞lim​m→∞lim​sn,m​=1 lim⁡m→∞lim⁡n→∞sn,m=0\lim_{m\to\infty}\lim_{n\to\infty}s_{n,m}=0m→∞lim​n→∞lim​sn,m​=0

We cannot always switch the order of limits. We cannot also do this on derivatives.

Definition 7.7

A sequence of functions {fn}\{f_n\}{fn​} converges uniformly to fff on set EEE if

∀ϵ>0,∃N,∀n≥N,∀x∈E,∣fn(x)−f(x)∣<ϵ\forall \epsilon>0, \exists N, \forall n\geq N, \forall x\in E, |f_n(x)-f(x)|<\epsilon∀ϵ>0,∃N,∀n≥N,∀x∈E,∣fn​(x)−f(x)∣<ϵ

If EEE is just a point, then it is the common definition of convergence.

If you have uniform convergence, then you can switch the order of limits.
