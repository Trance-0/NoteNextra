Online Algorithms

Example 1: Elevator

Problem: You’ve entered the lobby of a tall building, and want to go to the top floor as quickly as possible. There is an elevator which takes EEE time to get to the top once it arrives. You can also take the stairs which takes SSS time to climb (once you start) with S>ES>ES>E. However, you do not know when the elevator will arrive.

Offline (Clairvoyant) vs. Online

Offline: If you know that the elevator is arriving in TTT time, the what will you do?

  • Easy. I will computer E+TE+TE+T with SSS and take the smaller one.

Online: You do not know when the elevator will arrive.

  • You can either wait for the elevator or take the stairs.

Strategies

Always take the stairs.

Your cost SSS,

Optimal Cost: EEE.

Your cost / Optimal cost = SE\frac{S}{E}ES​.

SSS would be arbitrary large. For example, the Empire State Building has 103103103 floors.

Wait for the elevator

Your cost T+ET+ET+E

Optimal Cost: SSS (if TTT is large)

Your cost / Optimal cost = T+ES\frac{T+E}{S}ST+E​.

TTT could be arbitrary large. For out of service elevator, TTT could be infinite.

Online Algorithms

Definition: An online algorithm must take decisions without full information about the problem instance [in this case TTT] and/or it does not know the future [e.g. makes decision immediately as jobs come in without knowing the future jobs].

An offline algorithm has the full information about the problem instance.

Competitive Ratio

Quality of online algorithm is quantified by the competitive ratio (Idea is similar to the approximation ratio in optimization).

Consider a problem LLL (minimization) and let lll be an instance of this problem.

C∗(l)C^*(l)C∗(l) is the cost of the optimal offline solution with full information and unlimited computational power.

AAA is the online algorithm for LLL.

CA(l)C_A(l)CA​(l) is the value of AAA‘s solution on lll.

An online algorithm AAA is α\alphaα-competitive if

CA(l)C∗(l)≤α\frac{C_A(l)}{C^*(l)}\leq \alphaC∗(l)CA​(l)​≤α

for all instances lll of the problem.

In other words, α=max⁡lCA(l)C∗(l)\alpha=\max_l\frac{C_A(l)}{C^*(l)}α=maxl​C∗(l)CA​(l)​.

For maximization problems, we want to minimize the comparative ratio.

Back to the Elevator Problem

Strategy 1: Always take the stairs. Ratio is SE\frac{S}{E}ES​. can be arbitrarily large.

Strategy 2: Wait for the elevator. Ratio is T+ES\frac{T+E}{S}ST+E​. can be arbitrarily large.

Strategy 3: We do not make a decision immediately. Let’s wait for RRR times and then takes stairs if elevator does not arrive.

Question: What is the value of RRR? (how long to wait?)

Let’s try R=SR=SR=S.

Claim: The comparative ratio is 222.

Proof

Case 1: The optimal offline solution takes the elevator, then T+E≤ST+E\leq ST+E≤S.

We also take the elevator.

Competitive ratio = T+ET+E=1\frac{T+E}{T+E}=1T+ET+E​=1.

Case 2: The optimal offline solution takes the stairs, immediately.

We wait for RRR times and then take the stairs. In worst case, we wait for RRR times and then take the stairs for RRR.

Competitive ratio = 2RR=2\frac{2R}{R}=2R2R​=2.

Let’s try R=S−ER=S-ER=S−E instead.

Claim: The comparative ratio is max{1,2−ES}max\{1,2-\frac{E}{S}\}max{1,2−SE​}.

Proof

Case 1: The optimal offline solution takes the elevator, then T+E≤ST+E\leq ST+E≤S.

We also take the elevator.

Competitive ratio = T+ET+E=1\frac{T+E}{T+E}=1T+ET+E​=1.

Case 2: The optimal offline solution takes the stairs, immediately.

We wait for R=S−ER=S-ER=S−E times and then take the stairs.

Competitive ratio = S−E+SS=2−ES\frac{S-E+S}{S}=2-\frac{E}{S}SS−E+S​=2−SE​.

What if we wait less time? Let’s try R=S−E−ϵR=S-E-\epsilonR=S−E−ϵ for some ϵ>0\epsilon>0ϵ>0

In the worst case, we take the stairs for S−E−ϵS-E-\epsilonS−E−ϵ times and then take the stairs for SSS.

Competitive ratio = (S−E−ϵ)+SS−E−ϵ+E=2S−E−ϵ2S−E>2−ES\frac{(S-E-\epsilon)+S}{S-E-\epsilon+E}=\frac{2S-E-\epsilon}{2S-E}>2-\frac{E}{S}S−E−ϵ+E(S−E−ϵ)+S​=2S−E2S−E−ϵ​>2−SE​.

So the optimal competitive ratio is max{1,2−ES}max\{1,2-\frac{E}{S}\}max{1,2−SE​} when we wait for S−ES-ES−E time.

Example 2: Cache Replacement

Cache: Data in a cache is organized in blocks (also called pages or cache lines).

If CPU accesses data that is already in the cache, it is called cache hit, then access is fast.

If CPU accesses data that is not in the cache, it is called cache miss, This block if brought to cache from main memory. If the cache already has kkk blocks (full), then another block need to be kicked out (eviction).

Global: Minimize the number of cache misses.

Clairvoyant policy: Knows that will be accessed in the future and the sequence of access.

FIF: Farthest in the future

Example: k=3k=3k=3, cache has 333 blocks.

Sequence: ABCDCABA B C D C A BABCDCAB

Cache: ABCA B CABC, the evict BBB for DDD. then 3 warm up and 1 miss.

Online Algorithm: Least recently used (LRU)

LRU: least recently used.

Example: ABCDCABA B C D C A BABCDCAB

Cache: ABCA B CABC, the evict AAA for DDD. then 3 warm up and 1 miss.

Cache: DBCD B CDBC, the evict BBB for AAA. 1 miss.

Cache: DACD A CDAC, the evict DDD for BBB. 1 miss.

Competitive Ratio for LRU

Claim: LRU is k+1k+1k+1-competitive.

Proof

Split the sequence into subsequences such that each subsequence contains k+1k+1k+1 distinct blocks.

For example, suppose k=3k=3k=3, sequence ABCDCEFGEAABCDCEFGEAABCDCEFGEA, subsequences are ABCDCABCDCABCDC and EFGEAEFGEAEFGEA.

LRU Cache: In each subsequence, it has at most k+1k+1k+1 misses.

The optimal offline solution: In each subsequence, must have at least 111 miss.

So the competitive ratio is at most k+1k+1k+1.

Using similar analysis, we can show that LRU is kkk competitive.

Hint for the proof:

Split the sequence into subsequences such that each subsequence LRU has kkk misses.

Argue that OPT has at least 111 miss in each subsequence.

Many sensible algorithms are kkk-competitive

Lower Bound: No deterministic online algorithm is better than kkk-competitive.

Resource augmentation: Offline algorithm (which knows the future) has kkk cache lines in its cache and the online algorithm has ckckck cache lines with c>1c>1c>1.

Lemma: Competitive Ratio is ∼cc−1\sim \frac{c}{c-1}∼c−1c​

Say c=2c=2c=2. LRU cache has twice as much as cache. LRU is 222-competitive.

Proof

LRU has cache of size 2k2k2k.

Divide the sequence into subsequences such that you have ckckck distinct pages.

In each subsequence, LRU has at most ckckck misses.

The OPT has at least (c−1)k(c-1)k(c−1)k misses.

So competitive ratio is at most ck(c−1)k=cc−1\frac{ck}{(c-1)k}=\frac{c}{c-1}(c−1)kck​=c−1c​.

Actual competitive ratio is ∼cc−1+1k\sim \frac{c}{c-1+\frac{1}{k}}∼c−1+k1​c​.

Example 3: Pessimal cache problem

Maximize number of cache misses.

Maximization problem: competitive ratio is max{cost of the optimal online algorithmcost of our algorithm}max\{\frac{\text{cost of the optimal online algorithm}}{\text{cost of our algorithm}}\}max{cost of our algorithmcost of the optimal online algorithm​}.

Or get min{cost of our algorithmcost of the optimal online algorithm}min\{\frac{\text{cost of our algorithm}}{\text{cost of the optimal online algorithm}}\}min{cost of the optimal online algorithmcost of our algorithm​}.

The size of the cache is kkk.

So if OPT has XXX cache misses, we want ≥Xα\geq \frac{X}{\alpha}≥αX​. cache misses where α\alphaα is the competitive ratio.

Claim: The OPT could always miss (note quite) except when the page is accessed twice in a row.

Claim: No deterministic online algorithm has a bounded competitive ratio. (that is independent of the length of the sequence)

Proof:

Start with an empty cache. (size of cache is kkk)

Miss the first kkk unique pages.

P1,P2,⋯ ,Pk∣Pk+1,Pk+2,⋯ ,P2kP_1,P_2,\cdots,P_k|P_{k+1},P_{k+2},\cdots,P_{2k}P1​,P2​,⋯,Pk​∣Pk+1​,Pk+2​,⋯,P2k​

Say your deterministic online algorithm choose to evict PiP_iPi​ for i∈{1,2,⋯ ,k}i\in\{1,2,\cdots,k\}i∈{1,2,⋯,k}.

We want to choose PiP_iPi​ for i∈{1,2,⋯ ,k}i\in\{1,2,\cdots,k\}i∈{1,2,⋯,k} such that the number of misses is maximized.

The optimal offline solution: evict the page that will be accessed furthest in the future. Let’s call it σ\sigmaσ.

The online algorithm: evict PiP_iPi​ for i∈{1,2,⋯ ,k}i\in\{1,2,\cdots,k\}i∈{1,2,⋯,k}. Will have k+1k+1k+1 misses in the worst case.

So the competitive ratio is at most σk+1\frac{\sigma}{k+1}k+1σ​, which is unbounded.

Randomized most recently used (RAND, MRU)

MRU without randomization is a deterministic algorithm, and thus, the competitive ration is bounded.

First kkk unique accesses brings all pages to cache.

On the k+1k+1k+1th access, pick a random page from the cache and evict it.

After that evict the MRU no a miss.

Claim: RAND is kkk-competitive.

Lemma: After the first k+1k+1k+1 unique accesses at all times

  1. 1 page is in the cache with probability 1 (the MRU one)
  2. There exists kkk pages each of which is in the cache with probability 1−1k1-\frac{1}{k}1−k1​
  3. All other pages are in the cache with probability 000.

Proof

By induction.

Base case: right after the first k+1k+1k+1 unique accesses and before k+2k+2k+2th access.

  1. Pk+1P_{k+1}Pk+1​ is in the cache with probability 111.
  2. When we brought Pk+1P_{k+1}Pk+1​ to the cache, we evicted one page uniformly at random. (i.e. PiP_iPi​ is evicted with probability 1k\frac{1}{k}k1​, PiP_iPi​ is still in the cache with probability 1−1k1-\frac{1}{k}1−k1​)
  3. All other rrr pages are definitely not in the cache because we did not see them yet.

Inductive cases:

Let PPP be a page that is in the cache with probability 000

Cache miss and RAND MRU evict P′P'P′ for another page with probability in this cache with probability 000.

  1. PPP is in the cache with probability 111.
  2. By induction, there exists a set of kkk pages each of which is in the cache with probability 1−1k1-\frac{1}{k}1−k1​.
  3. All other pages are in the cache with probability 000.

Let PPP be a page in the cache with probability 1−1k1-\frac{1}{k}1−k1​.

With probability 1k\frac{1}{k}k1​, PPP is not in the cache and RAND evicts P′P'P′ in the cache and brings PPP to the cache.

MRU is kkk-competitive.

Proof

Case 1: Access MRU page.

Both OPT and our algorithm don’t miss.

Case 2: Access some other 1 page

OPT definitely misses.

RAND MRU misses with probability ≥1k\geq \frac{1}{k}≥k1​.
