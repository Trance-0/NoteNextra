New materials

Topology basis

Given a topology on a set XXX, When is a given collection of subsets of XXX a basis for a topology?

Suppose U∈TU\in\mathcal{T}U∈T is an open set in XXX. If an arbitrary set C\mathcal{C}C is a basis for T\mathcal{T}T, then by the definition of a topology generated by a basis, we should have the following:

∃C∈C such that x∈C⊆U\exists C\in \mathcal{C} \text{ such that } x\in C\subseteq U∃C∈C such that x∈C⊆U

Theorem of basis of topology

Caution

In this course, we use lowercase letters to denote element of a set, and uppercase letters to denote sets. We use X\mathcal{X}X to denote set of subsets of XXX.

Let (X,T)(X,\mathcal{T})(X,T) be a topological space. Let C⊆T\mathcal{C}\subseteq \mathcal{T}C⊆T be a collection of subsets of XXX satisfying the following property:

∀U∈T,∃C∈C such that C⊆U\forall U\in \mathcal{T}, \exists C\in \mathcal{C} \text{ such that } C\subseteq U∀U∈T,∃C∈C such that C⊆U

Then C\mathcal{C}C is a basis and the topology generated by C\mathcal{C}C is T\mathcal{T}T.

Proof

We want to show that C\mathcal{C}C is a basis.

Recall the definition of a basis:

  1. ∀x∈X\forall x\in X∀x∈X, there is B∈BB\in \mathcal{B}B∈B such that x∈Bx\in Bx∈B
  2. ∀B1,B2∈B\forall B_1,B_2\in \mathcal{B}∀B1​,B2​∈B, ∀x∈B1∩B2\forall x\in B_1\cap B_2∀x∈B1​∩B2​, there is B3∈BB_3\in \mathcal{B}B3​∈B such that x∈B3⊆B1∩B2x\in B_3\subseteq B_1\cap B_2x∈B3​⊆B1​∩B2​

First, we want to show that C\mathcal{C}C satisfies the first property.

Take x∈Xx\in Xx∈X. Since X∈TX\in \mathcal{T}X∈T, we can apply the given condition (∀U∈T,∃C∈C such that U⊆C\forall U\in \mathcal{T}, \exists C\in \mathcal{C} \text{ such that } U\subseteq C∀U∈T,∃C∈C such that U⊆C) to get C∈CC\in \mathcal{C}C∈C such that x∈C⊆Xx\in C\subseteq Xx∈C⊆X.

Next, we want to show that C\mathcal{C}C satisfies the second property.

Let C1,C2∈CC_1,C_2\in \mathcal{C}C1​,C2​∈C and x∈C1∩C2x\in C_1\cap C_2x∈C1​∩C2​. Since C1,C2∈TC_1,C_2\in \mathcal{T}C1​,C2​∈T, by the definition of T\mathcal{T}T, we have U=C1∩C2∈TU=C_1\cap C_2\in \mathcal{T}U=C1​∩C2​∈T.

We can apply the given condition to get C3∈CC_3\in \mathcal{C}C3​∈C such that x∈C3⊆U=C1∩C2x\in C_3\subseteq U=C_1\cap C_2x∈C3​⊆U=C1​∩C2​.

Then we want to show that the topology generated by C\mathcal{C}C is T\mathcal{T}T.

Recall the definition of the topology generated by a basis:

To prove this, we need to show that ∀U∈T  ⟹  U∈TC\forall U\in \mathcal{T}\implies U\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{C}}∀U∈T⟹U∈TC​ and ∀U∈TC  ⟹  U∈T\forall U\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{C}}\implies U\in \mathcal{T}∀U∈TC​⟹U∈T.

Moreover, from last lecture, we have U∈TB  ⟺  U=⋃α∈IBαU\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}\iff U=\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} B_\alphaU∈TB​⟺U=⋃α∈I​Bα​ for some {Bα}α∈I⊆B\{B_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in I}\subseteq \mathcal{B}{Bα​}α∈I​⊆B.

First, we want to show that ∀U∈TC  ⟹  U∈T\forall U\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{C}}\implies U\in \mathcal{T}∀U∈TC​⟹U∈T.

Let U=⋃α∈ICαU=\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} C_\alphaU=⋃α∈I​Cα​ for some {Cα}α∈I⊆C\{C_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in I}\subseteq \mathcal{C}{Cα​}α∈I​⊆C. Then since Cα∈TC_\alpha\in \mathcal{T}Cα​∈T, by the definition of T\mathcal{T}T, we have U∈TU\in \mathcal{T}U∈T.

Next, we want to show that ∀U∈T  ⟹  U∈TC\forall U\in \mathcal{T}\implies U\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{C}}∀U∈T⟹U∈TC​.

Let U∈TU\in \mathcal{T}U∈T. Then ∀x∈U\forall x\in U∀x∈U by the given condition, we have C∈CC\in \mathcal{C}C∈C such that x∈C⊆Ux\in C\subseteq Ux∈C⊆U.

So, U=⋃α∈ICα∈TCU=\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} C_\alpha\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{C}}U=⋃α∈I​Cα​∈TC​. (using the same trick last time )

Let T\mathcal{T}T be the topology on XXX. Then T\mathcal{T}T itself satisfies the basis condition.

Definition of subbasis of topology

A subbasis of a topology on a set XXX is a collection S⊆T\mathcal{S}\subseteq \mathcal{T}S⊆T of subsets of XXX such that their union is XXX.

S={Sα∣Sα⊆X}α∈I and ⋃α∈ISα=X\mathcal{S}=\{S_{\alpha}\mid S_\alpha\subseteq X\}_{\alpha \in I}\text{ and }\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} S_\alpha=XS={Sα​∣Sα​⊆X}α∈I​ and α∈I⋃​Sα​=X

Definition of topology generated by a subbasis

If we consider the basis generated by the subbasis S\mathcal{S}S by the following:

B={B∣B is the intersection of a finite number of elements of S}\mathcal{B}=\{B\mid B\text{ is the intersection of a finite number of elements of }\mathcal{S}\}B={B∣B is the intersection of a finite number of elements of S}

Then B\mathcal{B}B is a basis.

Proof

First, ∀x∈X\forall x\in X∀x∈X, there is Sα∈SS_\alpha\in \mathcal{S}Sα​∈S such that x∈Sαx\in S_\alphax∈Sα​. In particular, x∈Bx\in \mathcal{B}x∈B.

Second, let B1,B2∈BB_1,B_2\in \mathcal{B}B1​,B2​∈B. Since B1B_1B1​ is the intersection of a finite number of elements of S\mathcal{S}S, we have B1=⋂i=1nSi1,B2=⋂i=1nSi2B_1=\bigcap_{i=1}^n S_{i_1}, B_2=\bigcap_{i=1}^n S_{i_2}B1​=⋂i=1n​Si1​​,B2​=⋂i=1n​Si2​​ for some Si1,Si2∈SS_{i_1},S_{i_2}\in \mathcal{S}Si1​​,Si2​​∈S.

So B1∩B2B_1\cap B_2B1​∩B2​ is the intersection of finitely many elements of S\mathcal{S}S.

So B1∩B2∈BB_1\cap B_2\in \mathcal{B}B1​∩B2​∈B.

We call the topology generated by B\mathcal{B}B the topology generated by the subbasis S\mathcal{S}S. Denote it by TS\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{S}}TS​.

An open set with respect to TS\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{S}}TS​ is a subset of XXX such that it can be written as a union of finitely intersections of elements of S\mathcal{S}S.

Example (standard topology on real numbers)

Let X=RX=\mathbb{R}X=R. Take S={(−∞,a)∣a∈R}∪{(a,+∞)∣a∈R}\mathcal{S}=\{(-\infty, a)|a\in \mathbb{R}\}\cup \{(a,+\infty)|a\in \mathbb{R}\}S={(−∞,a)∣a∈R}∪{(a,+∞)∣a∈R}.

We claim this is a subbasis of the standard topology on R\mathbb{R}R.

The basis B\mathcal{B}B associated with S\mathcal{S}S is the collection of all open intervals.

B={(a,b)=(−∞,b)∩(a,+∞)}\mathcal{B}=\{(a,b)=(-\infty, b)\cap (a,+\infty)\}B={(a,b)=(−∞,b)∩(a,+∞)}

So, B=Bst\mathcal{B}=\mathcal{B}_{st}B=Bst​ (the standard basis).

This topology on R\mathbb{R}R is the same as the standard topology on R\mathbb{R}R.

Example (finite complement topology)

Let XXX be an arbitrary set. Let S\mathcal{S}S defined as follows:

S={S⊆X∣S=X∖{x} for some x∈X}\mathcal{S}=\{S\subseteq X\mid S=X\setminus \{x\} \text{ for some } x\in X\}S={S⊆X∣S=X∖{x} for some x∈X}

Let x,y∈Xx,y\in Xx,y∈X and x≠yx\neq yx=y. Then Sx=X∖{x}S_x=X\setminus \{x\}Sx​=X∖{x} and Sy=X∖{y}S_y=X\setminus \{y\}Sy​=X∖{y} are two elements of S\mathcal{S}S. Since x≠yx\neq yx=y, we have Sx∪Sy=X∖{x}∪X∖{y}=XS_x\cup S_y=X\setminus \{x\}\cup X\setminus \{y\}=XSx​∪Sy​=X∖{x}∪X∖{y}=X. So S\mathcal{S}S is a subbasis of XXX.

So, the basis associated with S\mathcal{S}S, B\mathcal{B}B, is the collection of subsets of XXX with finite complement.

This is in fact a topology, which is the finite complement topology on XXX.
