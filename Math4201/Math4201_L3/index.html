Basis for a topology

Let XXX be a set. A basis for a topology on XXX is a collection B\mathcal{B}B (elements of B\mathcal{B}B are called basis elements) of subsets of XXX such that:

  1. ∀x∈X\forall x\in X∀x∈X, ∃B∈B\exists B\in \mathcal{B}∃B∈B such that x∈Bx\in Bx∈B
  2. ∀B1,B2∈B\forall B_1,B_2\in \mathcal{B}∀B1​,B2​∈B, ∀x∈B1∩B2\forall x\in B_1\cap B_2∀x∈B1​∩B2​, ∃B3∈B\exists B_3\in \mathcal{B}∃B3​∈B such that x∈B3⊆B1∩B2x\in B_3\subseteq B_1\cap B_2x∈B3​⊆B1​∩B2​

Example of standard basis in real numbers

Let X=RX=\mathbb{R}X=R and B={(a,b)∣a,b∈R,a<b}\mathcal{B}=\{(a,b)|a,b\in \mathbb{R},a<b\}B={(a,b)∣a,b∈R,a<b} (collection of all open intervals).

Check properties 1:

for any x∈Rx\in \mathbb{R}x∈R, ∃(x−1,x+1)∈B\exists (x-1,x+1)\in \mathcal{B}∃(x−1,x+1)∈B such that x∈(x−1,x+1)x\in (x-1,x+1)x∈(x−1,x+1)

Check properties 2:

let B1=(a,b)B_1=(a,b)B1​=(a,b) and B2=(c,d)B_2=(c,d)B2​=(c,d) be two basis elements, and x∈B1∩B2=(max⁡(a,c),min⁡(b,d))∈Bx\in B_1\cap B_2=(\max(a,c),\min(b,d))\in \mathcal{B}x∈B1​∩B2​=(max(a,c),min(b,d))∈B.

Check properties 2:

let B1=[a,b)B_1=[a,b)B1​=[a,b) and B2=[c,d)B_2=[c,d)B2​=[c,d) be two basis elements, and x∈B1∩B2=[max(a,c),min(b,d))∈BLLx\in B_1\cap B_2=[max(a,c),min(b,d))\in \mathcal{B}_{LL}x∈B1​∩B2​=[max(a,c),min(b,d))∈BLL​.

Definition for cartesian product

Let XXX and YYY be sets. The cartesian product of XXX and YYY is the set X×Y={(x,y)∣x∈X,y∈Y}X\times Y=\{(x,y)|x\in X,y\in Y\}X×Y={(x,y)∣x∈X,y∈Y}.

Let X=R2X=\mathbb{R}^2X=R2 and B\mathcal{B}B be the collection of rectangle of the form (a,b)×(c,d)(a,b)\times (c,d)(a,b)×(c,d) where a,b,c,d∈Ra,b,c,d\in \mathbb{R}a,b,c,d∈R and a<b,c<da<b,c<da<b,c<d. (boundary is not included)

Topology generated by a basis

Let B\mathcal{B}B be a basis for a topology on XXX. The topology generated by B\mathcal{B}B, denoted by TB\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}TB​.

U∈TB  ⟺  ∀x∈U,∃B∈BU\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}\iff \forall x\in U, \exists B\in \mathcal{B}U∈TB​⟺∀x∈U,∃B∈B such that x∈B⊆Ux\in B\subseteq Ux∈B⊆U

Proof

TB\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}TB​ is a topology on XXX because:

  1. ∅∈TB\emptyset \in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}∅∈TB​ because ∅∈B\emptyset \in \mathcal{B}∅∈B. X∈TBX\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}X∈TB​ because ∀x∈X,∃B∈B\forall x\in X, \exists B\in \mathcal{B}∀x∈X,∃B∈B such that x∈B⊆Xx\in B\subseteq Xx∈B⊆X (by definition of basis (property 1)))

  2. TB\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}TB​ is closed under arbitrary unions.

    Want to show {Uα∣Uα∈TB}α∈I  ⟹  ⋃α∈IUα∈TB\{U_\alpha | U_\alpha\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}\}_{\alpha \in I}\implies \bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_\alpha\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}{Uα​∣Uα​∈TB​}α∈I​⟹⋃α∈I​Uα​∈TB​.

    Because ∀x∈⋃α∈IUα\forall x\in \bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_\alpha∀x∈⋃α∈I​Uα​, ∃α0\exists \alpha_0∃α0​ such that x∈Uα0x\in U_{\alpha_0}x∈Uα0​​. Since Uα0∈TBU_{\alpha_0}\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}Uα0​​∈TB​, ∃B∈B\exists B\in \mathcal{B}∃B∈B such that x∈B⊆Uα0⊆⋃α∈IUαx\in B\subseteq U_{\alpha_0}\subseteq \bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_\alphax∈B⊆Uα0​​⊆⋃α∈I​Uα​.

  3. TB\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}TB​ is closed under finite intersections.

    Want to show U1,U2,…,Un∈TB  ⟹  ⋂i=1nUi∈TBU_1,U_2,\ldots,U_n\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}\implies \bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}U1​,U2​,…,Un​∈TB​⟹⋂i=1n​Ui​∈TB​.

    If n=2n=2n=2, since ∀x∈U1∩U2\forall x\in U_1\cap U_2∀x∈U1​∩U2​, x∈U1x\in U_1x∈U1​ and x∈U2x\in U_2x∈U2​, ∃B1∈B\exists B_1\in \mathcal{B}∃B1​∈B such that x∈B1⊆U1x\in B_1\subseteq U_1x∈B1​⊆U1​ and ∃B2∈B\exists B_2\in \mathcal{B}∃B2​∈B.

    Applying the second property of basis, ∃B3∈B\exists B_3\in \mathcal{B}∃B3​∈B such that x∈B3⊆B1∩B2⊆U1∩U2x\in B_3\subseteq B_1\cap B_2\subseteq U_1\cap U_2x∈B3​⊆B1​∩B2​⊆U1​∩U2​.

    By induction, we can show that ⋂i=1nUi∈TB\bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}⋂i=1n​Ui​∈TB​.

Example of topology generated by a basis

Let XXX be arbitrary.

Let B={x∣x∈X}\mathcal{B}=\{x|x\in X\}B={x∣x∈X} (collection of all singleton subsets of XXX).

Then T\mathcal{T}T is the discrete topology.

Properties of basis in generated topology

Observation 1: Any B∈BB\in \mathcal{B}B∈B is an open set in TB\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}TB​.

By the defining property of basis, ∀x∈B\forall x\in B∀x∈B, ∃x∈B⊆B\exists x\in B\subseteq B∃x∈B⊆B.

Observation 2: For any collection {Bα}α∈I\{B_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in I}{Bα​}α∈I​, ⋃α∈IBα∈TB\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} B_\alpha\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}⋃α∈I​Bα​∈TB​.

By observation 1, each Bα∈TBB_\alpha\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}Bα​∈TB​. Since TB\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}TB​ is a topology, ⋃α∈IBα∈TB\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} B_\alpha\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}⋃α∈I​Bα​∈TB​.

Lemma

Let B\mathcal{B}B and TB\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}TB​ be a basis and the topology generated by B\mathcal{B}B on XXX. Then,

U∈TB  ⟺  U\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}\iffU∈TB​⟺ there are basis elements {Bα}α∈I\{B_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in I}{Bα​}α∈I​ such that U=⋃α∈IBαU=\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} B_\alphaU=⋃α∈I​Bα​.

Proof

(⇒)(\Rightarrow)(⇒)

If U∈TBU\in \mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}U∈TB​, we want to show that UUU is a union of basis elements.

For any x∈Ux\in Ux∈U, by the definition of TB\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}TB​, there is a basis element BxB_xBx​ such that x∈Bx⊆Ux\in B_x\subseteq Ux∈Bx​⊆U.

So, U⊆⋃x∈UBxU\subseteq \bigcup_{x\in U} B_xU⊆⋃x∈U​Bx​.

Since ∀Bx∈{Bx}α∈I\forall B_x\in \{B_x\}_{\alpha \in I}∀Bx​∈{Bx​}α∈I​, Bx⊆UB_x\subseteq UBx​⊆U, we have U⊇⋃x∈UBxU\supseteq\bigcup_{x\in U} B_xU⊇⋃x∈U​Bx​.

So, U=⋃x∈UBxU=\bigcup_{x\in U} B_xU=⋃x∈U​Bx​.

(⇐)(\Leftarrow)(⇐)

Applies observation 2.

Note

A basis for a topology is like a basis for a vector space in the sense that any open set/vector can be represented in terms of basis elements.

But unlike linear algebra, it’s not true that any open set can be written as a union of basis element in a unique way.
