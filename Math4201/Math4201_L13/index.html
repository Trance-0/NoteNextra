Metic spaces

Three different metrics on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn

Euclidean metric:

d(x,y)=∑i=1n(xi−yi)2d(x,y)=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i-y_i)^2}d(x,y)=i=1∑n​(xi​−yi​)2​

Square metric:

d(x,y)=max⁡i=1n∣xi−yi∣d(x,y)=\max_{i=1}^n |x_i-y_i|d(x,y)=i=1maxn​∣xi​−yi​∣

Manhattan metric:

d(x,y)=∑i=1n∣xi−yi∣d(x,y)=\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i-y_i|d(x,y)=i=1∑n​∣xi​−yi​∣

So to prove our proposition, we need to show that any pair of metrics ddd and d′d'd′ with basis generated by balls defined

B={Br(d)(x)∣x∈X,r>0,r∈R}\mathcal{B}=\{B_r^{(d)}(x)|x\in X,r>0,r\in \mathbb{R}\}B={Br(d)​(x)∣x∈X,r>0,r∈R}

and

B′={Br(d′)(x)∣x∈X,r>0,r∈R}\mathcal{B}'=\{B_r^{(d')}(x)|x\in X,r>0,r\in \mathbb{R}\}B′={Br(d′)​(x)∣x∈X,r>0,r∈R}

are equivalent.

Proposition: The metrics induce the same topology on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn

The three metrics induce the same topology on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, and it’s the standard topology.

Lemma of equivalent topologies

If T\mathcal{T}T and T′\mathcal{T}'T′ are two topologies on XXX, we say T\mathcal{T}T and T′\mathcal{T}'T′ are equivalent to each other if and only if the following two conditions are satisfied:

  1. ∀B1∈T,∃B2∈T′\forall B_1\in \mathcal{T}, \exists B_2\in \mathcal{T}'∀B1​∈T,∃B2​∈T′ such that ∀x∈B1,∃x∈B2⊆B1\forall x\in B_1, \exists x\in B_2\subseteq B_1∀x∈B1​,∃x∈B2​⊆B1​.
  2. ∀B2∈T′,∃B1∈T\forall B_2\in \mathcal{T}', \exists B_1\in \mathcal{T}∀B2​∈T′,∃B1​∈T such that ∀x∈B2,∃x∈B1⊆B2\forall x\in B_2, \exists x\in B_1\subseteq B_2∀x∈B2​,∃x∈B1​⊆B2​.

Lemma of equivalent metrics

Let ddd and d′d'd′ be two metrics on XXX. If the following holds, then the metric topology associated to ddd and d′d'd′ are equivalent.

  1. ∀x∈X,∀δ>0,∃ϵ>0\forall x\in X, \forall \delta>0, \exists \epsilon>0∀x∈X,∀δ>0,∃ϵ>0 such that Bδ(x)⊆Bϵ(x)B_\delta(x)\subseteq B_\epsilon(x)Bδ​(x)⊆Bϵ​(x)
  2. ∀x∈X,∀ϵ>0,∃δ>0\forall x\in X, \forall \epsilon>0, \exists \delta>0∀x∈X,∀ϵ>0,∃δ>0 such that Bϵ(x)⊆Bδ(x)B_\epsilon(x)\subseteq B_\delta(x)Bϵ​(x)⊆Bδ​(x)

Proof

To apply the lemma, we try to compute the three metrics on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn.

u=(u1,u2,…,un),v=(v1,v2,…,vn)∈Rnu=(u_1,u_2,\dots,u_n), v=(v_1,v_2,\dots,v_n)\in \mathbb{R}^nu=(u1​,u2​,…,un​),v=(v1​,v2​,…,vn​)∈Rn

For Euclidean metric:

d(u,v)=∑i=1n(ui−vi)2d(u,v)=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (u_i-v_i)^2}d(u,v)=i=1∑n​(ui​−vi​)2​

For square metric:

ρ(u,v)=max⁡i=1n∣ui−vi∣\rho(u,v)=\max_{i=1}^n |u_i-v_i|ρ(u,v)=i=1maxn​∣ui​−vi​∣

For Manhattan metric:

m(u,v)=∑i=1n∣ui−vi∣m(u,v)=\sum_{i=1}^n |u_i-v_i|m(u,v)=i=1∑n​∣ui​−vi​∣

First we will show that ddd and ρ\rhoρ are equivalent.

Note that

max⁡i=1n∣ui−vi∣≤∑i=1n(ui−vi)2\max_{i=1}^n |u_i-v_i|\leq \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (u_i-v_i)^2}i=1maxn​∣ui​−vi​∣≤i=1∑n​(ui​−vi​)2​

So ∀u∈Br(d)(x),d(u,v)<r  ⟹  ρ(u,v)<r\forall u\in B_r^{(d)}(x), d(u,v)<r\implies \rho(u,v)<r∀u∈Br(d)​(x),d(u,v)<r⟹ρ(u,v)<r

So u∈Br(ρ)(x)u\in B_r^{(\rho)}(x)u∈Br(ρ)​(x).

Br(d)(u)⊆Br(ρ)(u)B_r^{(d)}(u)\subseteq B_r^{(\rho)}(u)Br(d)​(u)⊆Br(ρ)​(u)

Note that

∑i=1n(ui−vi)2≤∑i=1nmax{∣ui−vi∣}2=n×max{∣ui−vi∣}\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (u_i-v_i)^2}\leq \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n max\{|u_i-v_i|\}^2}=\sqrt{n}\times max\{|u_i-v_i|\}i=1∑n​(ui​−vi​)2​≤i=1∑n​max{∣ui​−vi​∣}2​=n​×max{∣ui​−vi​∣}

So ∀u∈Br/n(ρ)(x),ρ(u,v)<r/n  ⟹  d(u,v)<r\forall u\in B_{r/\sqrt{n}}^{(\rho)}(x), \rho(u,v)<r/\sqrt{n}\implies d(u,v)<r∀u∈Br/n​(ρ)​(x),ρ(u,v)<r/n​⟹d(u,v)<r

So u∈Br(d)(x)u\in B_r^{(d)}(x)u∈Br(d)​(x).

So Br/n(ρ)(x)⊆Br(d)(x)B_{r/\sqrt{n}}^{(\rho)}(x)\subseteq B_r^{(d)}(x)Br/n​(ρ)​(x)⊆Br(d)​(x).

Finally, we will show that the topology generated by the square metric is the same as the product topology on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn.

Recall the basis for the product topology on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn with standard topology.

B={(a1,b1)×(a2,b2)×⋯×(an,bn)∣ai,bi∈R,ai<bi}\mathcal{B}=\{(a_1,b_1)\times (a_2,b_2)\times \cdots \times (a_n,b_n)|a_i,b_i\in \mathbb{R},a_i<b_i\}B={(a1​,b1​)×(a2​,b2​)×⋯×(an​,bn​)∣ai​,bi​∈R,ai​<bi​}

Let x=(x1,x2,…,xn)∈(a1,b1)×(a2,b2)×⋯×(an,bn)x=(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n)\in (a_1,b_1)\times (a_2,b_2)\times \cdots \times (a_n,b_n)x=(x1​,x2​,…,xn​)∈(a1​,b1​)×(a2​,b2​)×⋯×(an​,bn​).

Let δ=min⁡i=1n{∣xi−ai∣,∣xi−bi∣}\delta=\min_{i=1}^n \{|x_i-a_i|,|x_i-b_i|\}δ=mini=1n​{∣xi​−ai​∣,∣xi​−bi​∣}.

Then x∈Bδ(ρ)(x)⊆(a1,b1)×(a2,b2)×⋯×(an,bn)x\in B_\delta^{(\rho)}(x)\subseteq (a_1,b_1)\times (a_2,b_2)\times \cdots \times (a_n,b_n)x∈Bδ(ρ)​(x)⊆(a1​,b1​)×(a2​,b2​)×⋯×(an​,bn​).

In the other direction, let x∈Br(ρ)(x)x\in B_r^{(\rho)}(x)x∈Br(ρ)​(x), x=(x1,x2,…,xn)x=(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n)x=(x1​,x2​,…,xn​).

Then Br(ρ)(x)⊆(x1−r,x1+r)×(x2−r,x2+r)×⋯×(xn−r,xn+r)B_r^{(\rho)}(x)\subseteq (x_1-r,x_1+r)\times (x_2-r,x_2+r)\times \cdots \times (x_n-r,x_n+r)Br(ρ)​(x)⊆(x1​−r,x1​+r)×(x2​−r,x2​+r)×⋯×(xn​−r,xn​+r).

This is an element of B\mathcal{B}B, by combining the two directions, we have the standard topology is the same as the topology generated by the square metric.

Proposition of metric induced product topology

Let (X,d),(Y,d′)(X,d),(Y,d')(X,d),(Y,d′) be two metric spaces with metric topology T,T′\mathcal{T},\mathcal{T}'T,T′. On X×YX\times YX×Y, we can define a metric ρ\rhoρ by ρ((x,y),(x′,y′))≔max⁡{d(x,x′),d′(y,y′)}\rho((x,y),(x',y'))\coloneqq \max\{d(x,x'),d'(y,y')\}ρ((x,y),(x′,y′)):=max{d(x,x′),d′(y,y′)}, (x,y),(x′,y′)∈X×Y(x,y),(x',y')\in X\times Y(x,y),(x′,y′)∈X×Y.

Then this metric topology on X×YX\times YX×Y is the same as the product topology on X×YX\times YX×Y.

Note

Product of metrizable topological spaces is metrizable.
