Product topology

Define topological spaces on cartesian product of two topological spaces

Let (X,TX)(X,\mathcal{T}_X)(X,TX​) and (Y,TY)(Y,\mathcal{T}_Y)(Y,TY​) be two topological spaces.

X×Y={(x,y)∣x∈X,y∈Y}X\times Y=\{(x,y)|x\in X,y\in Y\}X×Y={(x,y)∣x∈X,y∈Y}

Goal: Define a topology on X×YX\times YX×Y.

One way is to take BX×Y={(U×V)∣U∈TX,V∈TY}\mathcal{B}_{X\times Y}=\{(U\times V)|U\in \mathcal{T}_X,V\in \mathcal{T}_Y\}BX×Y​={(U×V)∣U∈TX​,V∈TY​} is a basis for the topology on X×YX\times YX×Y.

There are two ways to define the topology on R2\mathbb{R}^2R2: By rectangles (Brect={(a,b)×(c,d)∣a,b,c,d∈R,a<b,c<d}\mathcal{B}_{rect}=\{(a,b)\times (c,d)|a,b,c,d\in \mathbb{R},a<b,c<d\}Brect​={(a,b)×(c,d)∣a,b,c,d∈R,a<b,c<d}) or by open disks (Bdisk={(x,y)∈R2∣d((x,y),(a,b))<r}\mathcal{B}_{disk}=\{(x,y)\in \mathbb{R}^2|d((x,y),(a,b))<r\}Bdisk​={(x,y)∈R2∣d((x,y),(a,b))<r}). (check bonus video)

Proof

Take U=X,V=YU=X,V=YU=X,V=Y, then (x,y)∈(U×V)=X×Y(x,y)\in (U\times V)=X\times Y(x,y)∈(U×V)=X×Y.

So the first property of basis is satisfied.

Check the second property of basis:

Let B1=U1×V1,B2=U2×V2B_1=U_1\times V_1,B_2=U_2\times V_2B1​=U1​×V1​,B2​=U2​×V2​ be two basis elements, and (x,y)∈B1∩B2(x,y)\in B_1\cap B_2(x,y)∈B1​∩B2​.

B1∩B2=(U1×V1)∩(U2×V2)=(U1∩U2)×(V1∩V2)B_1\cap B_2=(U_1\times V_1)\cap (U_2\times V_2)=(U_1\cap U_2)\times (V_1\cap V_2)B1​∩B2​=(U1​×V1​)∩(U2​×V2​)=(U1​∩U2​)×(V1​∩V2​)

Since U1∩U2∈TXU_1\cap U_2\in \mathcal{T}_XU1​∩U2​∈TX​ and V1∩V2∈TYV_1\cap V_2\in \mathcal{T}_YV1​∩V2​∈TY​, we have (U1∩U2)×(V1∩V2)∈BX×Y(U_1\cap U_2)\times (V_1\cap V_2)\in \mathcal{B}_{X\times Y}(U1​∩U2​)×(V1​∩V2​)∈BX×Y​.

Take B3=(U1∩U2)×(V1∩V2)B_3=(U_1\cap U_2)\times (V_1\cap V_2)B3​=(U1​∩U2​)×(V1​∩V2​), then (x,y)∈B3=B1∩B2(x,y)\in B_3=B_1\cap B_2(x,y)∈B3​=B1​∩B2​.

Caution

BX×Y\mathcal{B}_{X\times Y}BX×Y​ is not a topology on X×YX\times YX×Y.

BX×Y\mathcal{B}_{X\times Y}BX×Y​ is not closed with respect to arbitrary unions.

Definition of product topology

Let (X,TX)(X,\mathcal{T}_X)(X,TX​) and (Y,TY)(Y,\mathcal{T}_Y)(Y,TY​) be two topological spaces.

BX×Y={(U×V)∣U∈TX,V∈TY}\mathcal{B}_{X\times Y}=\{(U\times V)|U\in \mathcal{T}_X,V\in \mathcal{T}_Y\}BX×Y​={(U×V)∣U∈TX​,V∈TY​}

The product topology or X×YX\times YX×Y is the topology generated by BX×Y\mathcal{B}_{X\times Y}BX×Y​.

Let BX\mathcal{B}_XBX​ be a basis for TX\mathcal{T}_XTX​ and BY\mathcal{B}_YBY​ be a basis for TY\mathcal{T}_YTY​.

If we define

BX×Y′={(U×V)∣U∈BX,V∈BY}\mathcal{B}'_{X\times Y}=\{(U\times V)|U\in \mathcal{B}_X,V\in \mathcal{B}_Y\}BX×Y′​={(U×V)∣U∈BX​,V∈BY​}

Proposition

BX×Y′\mathcal{B}'_{X\times Y}BX×Y′​ is a basis for the product topology on X×YX\times YX×Y.

Note

This basis is smaller than BX×Y\mathcal{B}_{X\times Y}BX×Y​.

Consider X=Y=RX=Y=\mathbb{R}X=Y=R and U=(a,b)∩(c,d)U=(a,b)\cap (c,d)U=(a,b)∩(c,d) and V=(e,f)∩(g,h)V=(e,f)\cap (g,h)V=(e,f)∩(g,h). (Assume a<b,c<d,e<f,g<ha<b,c<d,e<f,g<ha<b,c<d,e<f,g<h) The union of UUU and VVV is four rectangles, which is not in BX×Y′\mathcal{B}'_{X\times Y}BX×Y′​. but it is in BX×Y\mathcal{B}_{X\times Y}BX×Y​.

Proof

Using the lemma  from Friday. it suffices to show that:

Let W∈X×YW\in X\times YW∈X×Y and (x,y)∈W(x,y)\in W(x,y)∈W, we need to show that there are B∈BX,B′∈BYB\in \mathcal{B}_X, B'\in \mathcal{B}_YB∈BX​,B′∈BY​ such that (x,y)∈(B×B′)⊆W(x,y)\in (B\times B')\subseteq W(x,y)∈(B×B′)⊆W.

Since WWW is open with respect to the topology generated by BX×Y\mathcal{B}_{X\times Y}BX×Y​, in particular, there is U×VU\times VU×V such that (x,y)∈U×V⊆W(x,y)\in U\times V\subseteq W(x,y)∈U×V⊆W. And x∈Ux\in Ux∈U and y∈Vy\in Vy∈V.

Since U∈BXU\in \mathcal{B}_XU∈BX​ and V∈BYV\in \mathcal{B}_YV∈BY​, by property of basis BX\mathcal{B}_XBX​ and BY\mathcal{B}_YBY​, ∀x∈U\forall x\in U∀x∈U, ∃B∈BX\exists B\in \mathcal{B}_X∃B∈BX​ such that x∈B⊆Ux\in B\subseteq Ux∈B⊆U and ∀y∈V\forall y\in V∀y∈V, ∃B′∈BY\exists B'\in \mathcal{B}_Y∃B′∈BY​ such that y∈B′⊆Vy\in B'\subseteq Vy∈B′⊆V.

So (x,y)∈(B×B′)⊆U×V⊆W(x,y)\in (B\times B')\subseteq U\times V\subseteq W(x,y)∈(B×B′)⊆U×V⊆W.

Definition of standard topology on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn

The standard topology on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn is defined as the product topology on Rn−1×R\mathbb{R}^{n-1}\times \mathbb{R}Rn−1×R.

Subspace topology

Let (X,T)(X,\mathcal{T})(X,T) be a topological space and Y⊆XY\subseteq XY⊆X.

Want to define a topology on YYY.

TY={U∩Y∣U∈T}\mathcal{T}_Y=\{U\cap Y|U\in \mathcal{T}\}TY​={U∩Y∣U∈T}

We claim that TY\mathcal{T}_YTY​ is a topology on YYY, called as the subspace topology on YYY.

Proof

First, ∅∩Y=∅∈TY\emptyset \cap Y=\emptyset \in \mathcal{T}_Y∅∩Y=∅∈TY​ and Y=X∩Y∈TYY=X\cap Y\in \mathcal{T}_YY=X∩Y∈TY​.

Second, let {Uα∩Y}α∈I\{U_\alpha\cap Y\}_{\alpha \in I}{Uα​∩Y}α∈I​ be collection of open sets in TY\mathcal{T}_YTY​. Note that Uα∈TU_\alpha\in \mathcal{T}Uα​∈T for all α∈I\alpha \in Iα∈I.

So, ⋃α∈IUα∩Y=(⋃α∈IUα)∩Y∈TY\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_\alpha\cap Y=\left(\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_\alpha\right)\cap Y\in \mathcal{T}_Y⋃α∈I​Uα​∩Y=(⋃α∈I​Uα​)∩Y∈TY​ because ⋃α∈IUα∈T\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_\alpha\in \mathcal{T}⋃α∈I​Uα​∈T.

Third, let {Ui∩Y}i=1n\{U_i\cap Y\}_{i=1}^n{Ui​∩Y}i=1n​ be a finite collection of open sets in TY\mathcal{T}_YTY​. Note that Ui∈TU_i\in \mathcal{T}Ui​∈T for all i=1,2,…,ni=1,2,\ldots,ni=1,2,…,n. So, ⋂i=1nUi∩Y=(⋂i=1nUi)∩Y∈TY\bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i\cap Y=\left(\bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i\right)\cap Y\in \mathcal{T}_Y⋂i=1n​Ui​∩Y=(⋂i=1n​Ui​)∩Y∈TY​ because ⋂i=1nUi∈T\bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i\in \mathcal{T}⋂i=1n​Ui​∈T.

Generate basis for subspace topology

Let B\mathcal{B}B is a basis for (X,T)(X,\mathcal{T})(X,T). We’d like to use B\mathcal{B}B to define a basis for (Y,TY)(Y,\mathcal{T}_Y)(Y,TY​).

BY={B∩Y∣B∈B}\mathcal{B}_Y=\{B\cap Y|B\in \mathcal{B}\}BY​={B∩Y∣B∈B}

Proposition for basis of subspace topology

BY\mathcal{B}_YBY​ is a basis for a topology on YYY that generates the subspace topology on YYY.

Proof as exercise. (same as the proof for the basis of product topology)

Example: not every open set in subspace topology is open in the original space

Let X=RX=\mathbb{R}X=R with standard topology and Y=[0,1]∪[2,3]Y=[0,1]\cup [2,3]Y=[0,1]∪[2,3]. equipped with subspace topology generated by the standard basis for R\mathbb{R}R.

so [0,1]=(−1,32)∩Y[0,1]=(-1,\frac{3}{2})\cap Y[0,1]=(−1,23​)∩Y In particular, [0,1][0,1][0,1] is open set in YYY, but not an open set in R\mathbb{R}R.

Lemma of open set in subspace topology

Let (X,T)(X,\mathcal{T})(X,T) be a topological space and Y⊆XY\subseteq XY⊆X is open. Z⊆YZ\subseteq YZ⊆Y is open subset with respect to the subspace topology on YYY. Then ZZZ is open in XXX.
