Connectedness and compactness of metric spaces

Connectedness and separation

Definition of separation

Let X=(X,T)X=(X,\mathcal{T})X=(X,T) be a topological space. A separation of XXX is a pair of open sets U,V∈TU,V\in \mathcal{T}U,V∈T that:

  1. U≠∅U\neq \emptysetU=∅ and V≠∅V\neq \emptysetV=∅ (that also equivalent to U≠XU\neq XU=X and V≠XV\neq XV=X)
  2. U∩V=∅U\cap V=\emptysetU∩V=∅
  3. X=U∪VX=U\cup VX=U∪V (∀x∈X\forall x\in X∀x∈X, x∈Ux\in Ux∈U or x∈Vx\in Vx∈V)

Some interesting corollary:

  • Any non-trivial (not ∅\emptyset∅ or XXX) clopen set can create a separation.
    • Proof: Let UUU be a non-trivial clopen set. Then UUU and UcU^cUc are disjoint open sets whose union is XXX.
  • For subspace Y⊂XY\subset XY⊂X, a separation of YYY is a pair of open sets U,V∈TYU,V\in \mathcal{T}_YU,V∈TY​ such that:
    1. U≠∅U\neq \emptysetU=∅ and V≠∅V\neq \emptysetV=∅ (that also equivalent to U≠YU\neq YU=Y and V≠YV\neq YV=Y)
    2. U∩V=∅U\cap V=\emptysetU∩V=∅
    3. Y=U∪VY=U\cup VY=U∪V (∀y∈Y\forall y\in Y∀y∈Y, y∈Uy\in Uy∈U or y∈Vy\in Vy∈V)
    • If A‾\overline{A}A is closure of AAA in XXX, same for B‾\overline{B}B, then the closure of AAA in YYY is A‾∩Y\overline{A}\cap YA∩Y and the closure of BBB in YYY is B‾∩Y\overline{B}\cap YB∩Y. Then for separation U,VU,VU,V of YYY, A‾∩B=A∩B‾=∅\overline{A}\cap B=A\cap \overline{B}=\emptysetA∩B=A∩B=∅.

Definition of connectedness

A topological space XXX is connected if there is no separation of XXX.

Tip

Connectedness is a local property. (That is, even the big space is connected, the subspace may not be connected. Consider R\mathbb{R}R with the usual metric. R\mathbb{R}R is connected, but R∖{0}\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}R∖{0} is not connected.)

Connectedness is a topological property. (That is, if XXX and YYY are homeomorphic, then XXX is connected if and only if YYY is connected. Consider if not, then separation of XXX gives a separation of YYY.)

Lemma of connected subspace

If A,BA,BA,B is a separation of a topological space XXX, and Y⊆XY\subseteq XY⊆X is a connected subspace with subspace topology, then YYY is either contained in AAA or BBB.

Easy to prove by contradiction. Try to construct a separation of YYY.

Theorem of connectedness of union of connected subsets

Let {Aα}α∈I\{A_\alpha\}_{\alpha\in I}{Aα​}α∈I​ be a collection of connected subsets of a topological space XXX such that ⋂α∈IAα\bigcap_{\alpha\in I} A_\alpha⋂α∈I​Aα​ is non-empty. Then ⋃α∈IAα\bigcup_{\alpha\in I} A_\alpha⋃α∈I​Aα​ is connected.

Easy to prove by lemma of connected subspace.

Lemma of compressing connectedness

Let A⊆XA\subseteq XA⊆X be a connected subspace of a topological space XXX and A⊆B⊆A‾A\subseteq B\subseteq \overline{A}A⊆B⊆A. Then BBB is connected.

Easy to prove by lemma of connected subspace. Suppose C,DC,DC,D is a separation of BBB, then AAA lies completely in either CCC or DDD. Without loss of generality, assume A⊆CA\subseteq CA⊆C. Then A‾⊆C‾\overline{A}\subseteq\overline{C}A⊆C and A‾∩D=∅\overline{A}\cap D=\emptysetA∩D=∅ (from C‾∩D=∅\overline{C}\cap D=\emptysetC∩D=∅ by closure of AAA). (contradiction that DDD is nonempty) So DDD is disjoint from A‾\overline{A}A, and hence from BBB. Therefore, BBB is connected.

Theorem of connected product space

Any finite cartesian product of connected spaces is connected.

Prove using the union of connected subsets theorem. Using fiber bundle like structure union with non-empty intersection.

Application of connectedness in real numbers

Real numbers are connected.

Using the least upper bound and greatest lower bound property, we can prove that any interval in real numbers is connected.

Intermediate Value Theorem

Let f:[a,b]→Rf:[a,b]\to \mathbb{R}f:[a,b]→R be continuous. If c∈Rc\in\mathbb{R}c∈R is such that f(a)<c<f(b)f(a)<c<f(b)f(a)<c<f(b), then there exists x∈[a,b]x\in [a,b]x∈[a,b] such that f(x)=cf(x)=cf(x)=c.

If false, then we can use the disjoint interval with projective map to create a separation of [a,b][a,b][a,b].

Definition of path-connected space

A topological space XXX is path-connected if for any two points x,x′∈Xx,x'\in Xx,x′∈X, there is a continuous map γ:[0,1]→X\gamma:[0,1]\to Xγ:[0,1]→X such that γ(0)=x\gamma(0)=xγ(0)=x and γ(1)=x′\gamma(1)=x'γ(1)=x′. Any such continuous map is called a path from xxx to x′x'x′.

  • Every connected space is path-connected.
    • The converse may not be true, consider the topologists’ sine curve.

Compactness

Compactness is a local property

Let XXX be a topological space. A subset Y⊆XY\subseteq XY⊆X is compact if and only if every open covering of YYY (set open in XXX) has a finite subcovering of YYY.

  • A space XXX is compact but the subspace may not be compact.
    • Consider X=[0,1]X=[0,1]X=[0,1] and Y=[0,1/2)Y=[0,1/2)Y=[0,1/2). YYY is not compact because the open cover {(0,1/n):n∈N}\{(0,1/n):n\in \mathbb{N}\}{(0,1/n):n∈N} does not have a finite subcover.
  • A compact subspace may live in a space that is not compact.
    • Consider X=RX=\mathbb{R}X=R and Y=[0,1]Y=[0,1]Y=[0,1]. YYY is compact but XXX is not compact.

Closed subspaces of compact spaces

A closed subspace of a compact space is compact.

A compact subspace of Hausdorff space is closed.

Each point not in the closed set have disjoint open neighborhoods with the closed set in Hausdorff space.

Theorem of compact subspaces with Hausdorff property

If YYY is compact subspace of a Hausdorff space XXX, x0∈X−Yx_0\in X-Yx0​∈X−Y, then there are disjoint open neighborhoods U,V⊆XU,V\subseteq XU,V⊆X such that x0∈Ux_0\in Ux0​∈U and Y⊆VY\subseteq VY⊆V.

Image of compact space under continuous map is compact

Let f:X→Yf:X\to Yf:X→Y be a continuous map and XXX is compact. Then f(X)f(X)f(X) is compact.

Tube lemma

Let X,YX,YX,Y be topological spaces and YYY is compact. Let N⊆X×YN\subseteq X\times YN⊆X×Y be an open set contains X×{y0}X\times \{y_0\}X×{y0​} for y0∈Yy_0\in Yy0​∈Y. Then there exists an open set W⊆YW\subseteq YW⊆Y is open containing y0y_0y0​ such that NNN contains X×WX\times WX×W.

Apply the finite intersection property of open sets in X×YX\times YX×Y. Projection map is continuous.

Product of compact spaces is compact

Let X,YX,YX,Y be compact spaces, then X×YX\times YX×Y is compact.

Any finite product of compact spaces is compact.

Compact subspaces of real numbers

Every closed and bounded subset of real numbers is compact

[a,b][a,b][a,b] is compact in R\mathbb{R}R with standard topology.

Good news for real numbers

Any of the three properties is equivalent for subsets of real numbers (product of real numbers):

  1. A⊆RnA\subseteq \mathbb{R}^nA⊆Rn is closed and bounded (with respect to the standard metric or spherical metric on Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn).
  2. A⊆RnA\subseteq \mathbb{R}^nA⊆Rn is compact.

Theorem of uniform continuous function

Let f:X→Yf:X\to Yf:X→Y be a continuous map between two metric spaces. If XXX is compact, then fff is uniformly continuous.

Definition of isolated point

A point x∈Xx\in Xx∈X is an isolated point if {x}\{x\}{x} is an open subset of XXX.

Theorem of isolated point in compact spaces

Let XXX be a nonempty compact Hausdorff space. If XXX has no isolated points, then XXX is uncountable.

Proof using infinite nested closed intervals should be nonempty.

Variation of compactness

Limit point compactness

A topological space XXX is limit point compact if every infinite subset of XXX has a limit point in XXX.

  • Every compact space is limit point compact.

Sequentially compact

A topological space XXX is sequentially compact if every sequence in XXX has a convergent subsequence.

  • Every compact space is sequentially compact.

Equivalence of three in metrizable spaces

If XXX is a metrizable space, then the following are equivalent:

  1. XXX is compact.
  2. XXX is limit point compact.
  3. XXX is sequentially compact.
