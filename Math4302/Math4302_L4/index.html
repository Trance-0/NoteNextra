Group Isomorphism

Definition of isomorphism

Let (G1,∗1)(G_1,*_1)(G1​,∗1​) and (G2,∗2)(G_2,*_2)(G2​,∗2​) be two groups. Then (G1,∗1)(G_1,*_1)(G1​,∗1​) and (G2,∗2)(G_2,*_2)(G2​,∗2​) are isomorphic if there exists a bijection f:G1→G2f:G_1\to G_2f:G1​→G2​ such that for all x,y∈G1x,y\in G_1x,y∈G1​, f(x∗y)=f(x)∗f(y)f(x*y)=f(x)*f(y)f(x∗y)=f(x)∗f(y). We say that (G1,∗1)(G_1,*_1)(G1​,∗1​) is isomorphic to (G2,∗2)(G_2,*_2)(G2​,∗2​).

(G1,∗1)≃(G2,∗2)(G_1,*_1)\simeq (G_2,*_2)(G1​,∗1​)≃(G2​,∗2​)

Example and non-example for isomorphism

As we have seen in class, (Z4,+)(\mathbb{Z}_4,+)(Z4​,+) and ({1,−1,i,−i},∗)(\{1,-1,i,-i\},*)({1,−1,i,−i},∗) are isomorphic.

(Z,+)(\mathbb{Z},+)(Z,+) and (R,+)(\mathbb{R},+)(R,+) are not isomorphic. There is no bijection from (Z,+)(\mathbb{Z},+)(Z,+) to (R,+)(\mathbb{R},+)(R,+).

Let M2(R)M_2(\mathbb{R})M2​(R) denotes the set of 2×22\times 22×2 matrices with addition. Then (R4,+)(\mathbb{R}^4,+)(R4,+) and (M2(R),+)(M_2(\mathbb{R}),+)(M2​(R),+) are isomorphic.

Isomorphism of Groups defines an equivalence relation

Isomorphism of groups is an equivalence relation.

  1. Reflexive: (G1,∗1)≃(G1,∗1)(G_1,*_1)\simeq (G_1,*_1)(G1​,∗1​)≃(G1​,∗1​)
  2. Symmetric: (G1,∗1)≃(G2,∗2)  ⟹  (G2,∗2)≃(G1,∗1)(G_1,*_1)\simeq (G_2,*_2)\implies (G_2,*_2)\simeq (G_1,*_1)(G1​,∗1​)≃(G2​,∗2​)⟹(G2​,∗2​)≃(G1​,∗1​)
  3. Transitive: (G1,∗1)≃(G2,∗2)∧(G2,∗2)≃(G3,∗3)  ⟹  (G1,∗1)≃(G3,∗3)(G_1,*_1)\simeq (G_2,*_2)\land (G_2,*_2)\simeq (G_3,*_3)\implies (G_1,*_1)\simeq (G_3,*_3)(G1​,∗1​)≃(G2​,∗2​)∧(G2​,∗2​)≃(G3​,∗3​)⟹(G1​,∗1​)≃(G3​,∗3​)

Easy to prove using bijective maps and definition of isomorphism.
