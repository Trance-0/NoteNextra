### Special case of Seifert-Van Kampen Theorem

Suppose $X=U\cup V$, where $U$ and $V$ are open subsets of $X$. Suppose that $U\cap V$ is path connected, and $x\in U\cap V$. Let $i,j$ be the inclusion maps of $U$ and $V$ into $X$, then the image of the induced homomorphism:

$$
i_*:\pi_1(U)\to\pi_1(X),\quad j_*:\pi_1(V)\to\pi_1(X)
$$

We proceed by cutting the intersecting region of the hemisphere cap into finite cover segments and rewire the hemisphere cap region with stereographic projection, therefore the fundamental group of $U\cap V$ is trivial since $\mathbb R^2-\{0\}$ is simply connected.

The Jordan curve theorem examine what happens when $U\cap V$ is not path connected.

### The Jordan Curve Theorem

Let $X$ be the union of two open sets $U$ and $V$ ($X=U\sqcup V$), such that $U\cap V$ can be written as the union of two disjoint open sets $A$ and $B$ ($U\cap V=A\sqcup B$). Assume that there is a path $\alpha\in U$ from a point $a$ of $A$ to a point $b$ of $B$, and that there is a path $\beta$ in $V$ from $b$ to $a$.

Let $f$ be the loop $f=a*b$.

1. The path-homotopy class $[f]$ generates an infinite cyclic subgroup of $\pi_1*(X,a)$.
2. If $\pi_1(X,a)$ is infinite cyclic, it is generated by $[f]$.
3. Assume there is a path $\gamma$ in $U$ from $a$ to the point $a'$ in $A$, and there is a path $\delta$ in $V$ from $a'$ to $a$. Let $g$ e the loop $g=\gamma *\delta$. Then the subgroups of $\pi_1(X,a)$ generated by $[f]$ and $[g]$ intersects in the identity element alone.

Recall that when we compute the fundamental group of the circle, we use appropriate covering space $E$ for the space $X$.

First we need to construct $E$, we proceed by pasting together copies of the subspaces $U$ and $V$. Let us take countably many copies of $U$ and countably many copies of $V$, all disjoint. (We label the $U_i$'s using even numbers $2i$, and label $V_i$'s using odd numbers $2i+1$.) 

We then have $\forall n\in \mathbb Z$, we have copies

$$
U\times (2n)\quad\text{ and }\quad  V\times (2n+1)
$$

Let $Y$ denote the union of these copies, $Y$ is the subspace of $X\times \mathbb Z$.

Now we form a new space $E$ as a quotient of $Y$ by identifying the points

$$
x\times (2n) \quad \text{ and } \quad x\times (2n-1)\quad \forall x\in A
$$

And by identifying the points

$$
x\times (2n) \quad \text{ and } \quad x\times (2n+1)\quad \forall x\in B
$$

Let $\pi:Y\to E$ be the quotient map.

Now the map $\rho:Y\to X$ defined by $\rho(x\times m)=x$ induces a map $p:E\to X$; the map $p$ is continuous because $E$ has the quotient topology. The map $p$ is also surjective. We shall show that $p$ is a covering map.
