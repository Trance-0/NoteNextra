Recall from previous lecture, we have unique lift for covering map.

Lemma for unique lifting homotopy for covering map

Let p:E→Bp: E\to Bp:E→B be a covering map, and e0∈Ee_0\in Ee0​∈E and p(e0)=b0p(e_0)=b_0p(e0​)=b0​. Let F:I×I→BF:I\times I\to BF:I×I→B be continuous with F(0,0)=b0F(0,0)=b_0F(0,0)=b0​. There is a unique lifting of FFF to a continuous map F~:T×I→E\tilde{F}:T\times I\to EF~:T×I→E, such that F~(0,0)=e0\tilde{F}(0,0)=e_0F~(0,0)=e0​.

Further more, if FFF is a path homotopy, then F~\tilde{F}F~ is a path homotopy.

Suppose f,gf,gf,g are two paths in BBB, and suppose fff and ggg are path homotopy (f(0)=g(0)=b0f(0)=g(0)=b_0f(0)=g(0)=b0​, and f(1)=g(1)=b1f(1)=g(1)=b_1f(1)=g(1)=b1​, b0,b1∈Bb_0,b_1\in Bb0​,b1​∈B), then f^:π1(B,b0)→π1(B,b1)\hat{f}:\pi_1(B,b_0)\to \pi_1(B,b_1)f^​:π1​(B,b0​)→π1​(B,b1​) and g^:π1(B,b0)→π1(B,b1)\hat{g}:\pi_1(B,b_0)\to \pi_1(B,b_1)g^​:π1​(B,b0​)→π1​(B,b1​) are path homotopic.

Since f,gf,gf,g are path homotopic, then there exists F:I×I→BF:I\times I\to BF:I×I→B such that

Let p:E→Bp: E\to Bp:E→B be a covering map, and p−1(b0)⊆Ep^{-1}(b_0)\subseteq Ep−1(b0​)⊆E be the fiber of b0b_0b0​.

ϕ([f])=f~(1)\phi([f])=\tilde{f}(1)ϕ([f])=f~​(1), and f~(0)=e0\tilde{f}(0)=e_0f~​(0)=e0​. Note that p(f~(1))=p(f(1))=b0p(\tilde{f}(1))=p(f(1))=b_0p(f~​(1))=p(f(1))=b0​.

Theorem for surjective lifting correspondence

Let ϕ:π1(E,b0)→p−1(b0)\phi:\pi_1(E,b_0)\to p^{-1}(b_0)ϕ:π1​(E,b0​)→p−1(b0​) be a lifting correspondence. If EEE is path connected, then ϕ\phiϕ is surjective.

Theorem for bijective lifting correspondence

Let ϕ:π1(E,b0)→p−1(b0)\phi:\pi_1(E,b_0)\to p^{-1}(b_0)ϕ:π1​(E,b0​)→p−1(b0​) be a lifting correspondence.

If EEE is simply connected, then ϕ\phiϕ is a bijection.

Proof

By previous theorem, it is sufficient to show that ϕ\phiϕ is one-to-one (i.e., ϕ\phiϕ is injective).

Suppose ϕ([f])=ϕ([g])\phi([f])=\phi([g])ϕ([f])=ϕ([g]), then f,g∈π1(E,b0)f,g\in \pi_1(E,b_0)f,g∈π1​(E,b0​) [f],[g]∈π1(B,b0)[f],[g]\in \pi_1(B,b_0)[f],[g]∈π1​(B,b0​) (ϕ\phiϕ is defined on loops in BBB, not EEE). So f~,g~:I→E\tilde{f},\tilde{g}:I\to Ef~​,g~​:I→E are path homotopic.

Why path homotopic: by definition f~(0)=g~(0)=e0\tilde f(0)=\tilde g(0)=e_0f~​(0)=g~​(0)=e0​, and the assumption ϕ([f])=ϕ([g])\phi([f])=\phi([g])ϕ([f])=ϕ([g]) gives f~(1)=g~(1)=:e0ˉ\tilde f(1)=\tilde g(1)=:\bar{e_0}f~​(1)=g~​(1)=:e0​ˉ​. So ∃F~:I×I→E\exists \tilde{F}:I\times I\to E∃F~:I×I→E such that

  • F~(s,0)=e0\tilde{F}(s,0)=e_0F~(s,0)=e0​
  • F~(s,1)=e0ˉ\tilde{F}(s,1)=\bar{e_0}F~(s,1)=e0​ˉ​
  • F~(0,t)=f~(t)\tilde{F}(0,t)=\tilde{f}(t)F~(0,t)=f~​(t)
  • F~(1,t)=g~(t)\tilde{F}(1,t)=\tilde{g}(t)F~(1,t)=g~​(t)

Define F=p∘F~:I×I→BF=p\circ \tilde{F}:I\times I\to BF=p∘F~:I×I→B, then

  • F(s,0)=p(e0)=b0F(s,0)=p(e_0)=b_0F(s,0)=p(e0​)=b0​
  • F(s,1)=p(e0ˉ)=b0F(s,1)=p(\bar{e_0})=b_0F(s,1)=p(e0​ˉ​)=b0​
  • F(0,t)=f(t)F(0,t)=f(t)F(0,t)=f(t)
  • F(1,t)=g(t)F(1,t)=g(t)F(1,t)=g(t)

So FFF is a path homotopy from fff to ggg in BBB: the t=0t=0t=0 and t=1t=1t=1 slices are constant at b0b_0b0​, and the s=0,1s=0,1s=0,1 slices are fff and ggg respectively.

Therefore [f]=[g][f]=[g][f]=[g], which shows that ϕ\phiϕ is injective. Combined with surjectivity from the previous theorem, ϕ\phiϕ is a bijection. □\square□

Theorem for fundamental group for circle

Let E=RE=\mathbb{R}E=R and B=S1B=S^1B=S1. Then ϕ:π1(E,b0)→π1(B,b0)≃Z\phi:\pi_1(E,b_0)\to \pi_1(B,b_0)\simeq \mathbb{Z}ϕ:π1​(E,b0​)→π1​(B,b0​)≃Z. is a isomorphism.

(fundamental group for circle is Z\mathbb{Z}Z)

Proof

Since R\mathbb{R}R is simply connected, then ϕ\phiϕ is a bijection.

It is suffice to show that ϕ\phiϕ satisfies the definition of homomorphism. ϕ([f]∗[g])=ϕ([f])+ϕ([g])\phi([f]*[g])=\phi([f])+\phi([g])ϕ([f]∗[g])=ϕ([f])+ϕ([g]).

Suppose f,g∈π1(S1,b0)f,g\in \pi_1(S^1,b_0)f,g∈π1​(S1,b0​), then ∃f~,g~:S1→R\exists \tilde{f},\tilde{g}:S^1\to \mathbb{R}∃f~​,g~​:S1→R such that ϕ([f])=n\phi([f])=nϕ([f])=n, ϕ([g])=m\phi([g])=mϕ([g])=m, then f~:S1→R\tilde{f}:S^1\to \mathbb{R}f~​:S1→R and g~:S1→R\tilde{g}:S^1\to \mathbb{R}g~​:S1→R such that

  • f~(0)=0\tilde{f}(0)=0f~​(0)=0
  • f~(1)=n\tilde{f}(1)=nf~​(1)=n
  • g~(0)=0\tilde{g}(0)=0g~​(0)=0
  • g~(1)=m\tilde{g}(1)=mg~​(1)=m

Take g~~(x)=g~(x)+n\tilde{\tilde{g}}(x)=\tilde{g}(x)+ng~​~​(x)=g~​(x)+n, then ϕ([f]∗[g])=ϕ(g~~)=m+n\phi([f]*[g])=\phi(\tilde{\tilde{g}})=m+nϕ([f]∗[g])=ϕ(g~​~​)=m+n.
