This is a review for definitions we covered in the classes. It may serve as a cheat sheet for the exam if you are allowed to use it.

Few important definitions

Quotient spaces

Let XXX be a topological space and f:X→Yf:X\to Yf:X→Y is a

  1. continuous
  2. surjective map.
  3. With the property that U⊂YU\subset YU⊂Y is open if and only if f−1(U)f^{-1}(U)f−1(U) is open in XXX.

Then we say fff is a quotient map and YYY is a quotient space.

Theorem of quotient space

Let p:X→Yp:X\to Yp:X→Y be a quotient map, let ZZZ be a space and g:X→Zg:X\to Zg:X→Z be a map that is constant on each set p−1(y)p^{-1}(y)p−1(y) for each y∈Yy\in Yy∈Y.

Then ggg induces a map f:X→Zf: X\to Zf:X→Z such that f∘p=gf\circ p=gf∘p=g.

The map fff is continuous if and only if ggg is continuous; fff is a quotient map if and only if ggg is a quotient map.

Algebraic topology

Manifold

Definition of Manifold

An mmm-dimensional manifold is a topological space XXX that is

  1. Hausdorff: every two distinct points of XXX have disjoint neighborhoods
  2. Second countable: With a countable basis
  3. Local euclidean: Each point of xxx of XXX has a neighborhood that is homeomorphic to an open subset of Rm\mathbb{R}^mRm.

Example of space that is not a manifold but satisfies part of the definition

Non-hausdorff:

Consider the set with two origin R∖{0}\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}R∖{0}. with {p,q}\{p,q\}{p,q}, and the topology defined over all the open intervals that don’t contain the origin, with set of the form (−a,0)∪{p}∪(0,a)(-a,0)\cup \{p\}\cup (0,a)(−a,0)∪{p}∪(0,a) for a∈Ra\in \mathbb{R}a∈R and (−a,0)∪{q}∪(0,a)(-a,0)\cup \{q\}\cup (0,a)(−a,0)∪{q}∪(0,a).

Non-second-countable:

Consider the long line R×[0,1)\mathbb{R}\times [0,1)R×[0,1)

Non-local-euclidean:

Any 1-dimensional CW complex (graph) that has a vertex with 3 or more edges connected to it will be Hausdorff and second-countable, but not locally Euclidean at those vertices.

Whitney’s Embedding Theorem

If XXX is a compact mmm-manifold, then XXX can be imbedded in RN\mathbb{R}^NRN for some positive integer NNN.

In general, XXX is not required to be compact. And NNN is not too big. For non compact XXX, N≤2m+1N\leq 2m+1N≤2m+1 and for compact XXX, N≤2mN\leq 2mN≤2m.

Definition of paracompact space

Locally finite: ∀x∈X\forall x\in X∀x∈X, ∃\exists∃ open x∈Ux\in Ux∈U such that UUU only intersects finitely many open sets in B\mathcal{B}B.

A space XXX is paracompact if every open cover AAA of XXX has a locally finite refinement B\mathcal{B}B of AAA that covers XXX.

Homotopy

Definition of homotopy equivalent spaces

Let f:X→Yf:X\to Yf:X→Y and g:X→Yg:X\to Yg:X→Y be tow continuous maps from a topological space XXX to a topological space YYY.

f∘g:Y→Yf\circ g:Y\to Yf∘g:Y→Y should be homotopy to IdYId_YIdY​ and g∘f:X→Xg\circ f:X\to Xg∘f:X→X should be homotopy to IdXId_XIdX​.

Definition of homotopy

Let f:X→Yf:X\to Yf:X→Y and g:X→Yg:X\to Yg:X→Y be tow continuous maps from a topological space XXX to a topological space YYY.

If there exists a continuous map F:X×[0,1]→YF:X\times [0,1]\to YF:X×[0,1]→Y such that F(x,0)=f(x)F(x,0)=f(x)F(x,0)=f(x) and F(x,1)=g(x)F(x,1)=g(x)F(x,1)=g(x) for all x∈Xx\in Xx∈X, then fff and ggg are homotopy equivalent.

Definition of null homology

If f:X→Yf:X\to Yf:X→Y is homotopy to a constant map. fff is called null homotopy.

Definition of path homotopy

Covering space

Definition of partition into slice

Let p:E→Bp:E\to Bp:E→B be a continuous surjective map. The open set U⊆BU\subseteq BU⊆B is said to be evenly covered by ppp if it’s inverse image p−1(U)p^{-1}(U)p−1(U) can be written as the union of disjoint open sets VαV_\alphaVα​ in EEE. Such that for each α\alphaα, the restriction of ppp to VαV_\alphaVα​ is a homeomorphism of VαV_\alphaVα​ onto UUU.

The collection of {Vα}\{V_\alpha\}{Vα​} is called a partition p−1(U)p^{-1}(U)p−1(U) into slice.

Definition of covering space

Let p:E→Bp:E\to Bp:E→B be a continuous surjective map.

If every point bbb of BBB has a neighborhood evenly covered by ppp, which means p−1(U)p^{-1}(U)p−1(U) is a union of disjoint open sets, then ppp is called a covering map and EEE is called a covering space.

Theorem exponential map gives covering map

The map p:R→S1p:\mathbb{R}\to S^1p:R→S1 defined by x↦e2πixx\mapsto e^{2\pi ix}x↦e2πix or (cos⁡(2πx),sin⁡(2πx))(\cos(2\pi x),\sin(2\pi x))(cos(2πx),sin(2πx)) is a covering map.

Definition of local homeomorphism

A continuous map p:E→Bp:E\to Bp:E→B is called a local homeomorphism if for every e∈Ee\in Ee∈E (note that for covering map, we choose b∈Bb\in Bb∈B), there exists a neighborhood UUU of bbb such that p∣U:U→p(U)p|_U:U\to p(U)p∣U​:U→p(U) is a homeomorphism on to an open subset p(U)p(U)p(U) of BBB.

Obviously, every open map induce a local homeomorphism. (choose the open disk around p(e)p(e)p(e))

Let p:E→Bp: E\to Bp:E→B be a covering map. If B0B_0B0​ is a subset of BBB, the map p∣p−1(B0):p−1(B0)→B0p|_{p^{-1}(B_0)}: p^{-1}(B_0)\to B_0p∣p−1(B0​)​:p−1(B0​)→B0​ is a covering map.

Theorem for product of covering map

If p:E→Bp:E\to Bp:E→B and p′:E′→B′p':E'\to B'p′:E′→B′ are covering maps, then p×p′:E×E′→B×B′p\times p':E\times E'\to B\times B'p×p′:E×E′→B×B′ is a covering map.
